# NLP Language Patterns: the Copywriting Engine

Milton Model (hypnotic vagueness), Meta Model (precision), presuppositions, embedded commands, Sleight of
Mouth (objection reframes), reframing, anchoring, submodalities. This is where copy gets its subconscious bite.

---

## 1. The Milton Model (artfully vague, bypasses the critical filter)
The inverse of precision language. Deliberately vague so the reader fills the gaps with THEIR own content,
making the message uniquely persuasive to each reader. The core of suggestive sales copy.

### A. Pacing & Leading (master structure)
Three undeniable truths (pace) → one statement that moves them (lead).
> "You've tried other tools. You've been let down before. You're cautious now, and that caution is exactly
> why you'll appreciate what comes next."

### B. Embedded Commands
A command hidden in a larger sentence, set off by emphasis (bold, italics, line break, button). Conscious mind
reads the sentence; subconscious receives the command. Command verbs: get, become, experience, remember, feel,
notice, discover, imagine, find, realise, decide, act.
- "As you read this, you may **start to feel excited** about the possibilities."
- "Most people who **sign up now** never look back."
- CTA button text IS an embedded command: "Start Free", "Get Instant Access", "Claim Your Spot".

**Weasel phrases (bolt a command onto each):**
1. **When you…** → "When you start saving 10 hours a week…"
2. **What would it be like if…** → "What would it be like if your inbox managed itself?"
3. **A person can…** (deflects resistance) → "A person can completely transform their workflow in a weekend."
4. **As you…** → "As you explore the demo, notice how fast everything feels."
5. **It's not necessary to…** (removes pressure, command still lands) → "It's not necessary to decide now, as you picture the result."
6. **You might find…** → "You might find that the more you use it, the more you wonder how you coped before."
7. **How surprised would you be to…** → "How surprised would you be to find your first results within 24 hours?"
8. **Invite you to notice…** → "I invite you to notice how relieved you feel just imagining this handled."

### C. Presuppositions (assume it, don't argue it) — most useful pattern for conversion
Build the desired belief into the grammar so it's never up for debate. The Hooka taxonomy uses 4 types:
- **Temporal** (when, as soon as, after, before, while): "Before you choose your plan…" "After you've set this up…"
- **Causal** (because, therefore, due to, since): "Because you care about quality, you'll appreciate…"
- **Identity** (people who…, real…): "People who take their craft seriously use…" "For real operators…"
- **Possession** (your, what you lack): "Your new workflow…" "The one thing standing between you and X…"
Plus: ordinals ("the first thing you'll notice…"), awareness verbs ("you'll quickly realise how much you save"),
adverbs ("how easily can you imagine this working for you").

### D. Mind Reading
Voice the reader's exact inner monologue. "You're probably wondering if this works for your business."
"I know exactly how frustrating this is." Must be ACCURATE to the ICP or it backfires.

### E. Cause-Effect (linkage)
because, as, so, makes, causes, the more…the more, since. "The more you use it, the more it learns."
Installs causal beliefs the reader doesn't audit.

### F. Tag Questions (lower resistance, invite yes)
Append "…isn't it? / …don't you? / …right? / …wouldn't you agree?" "That makes sense, doesn't it?"

### G. Double Binds (illusion of choice, either answer is a yes)
"Would you like to start monthly or annually?" "Do you want the Pro or the Team plan?" Both presuppose the buy.
Use on pricing pages and CTAs.

### H. Ambiguity & Nominalizations
Vague nouns (freedom, success, results, transformation) let each reader download their own meaning. Powerful for
aspirational copy; back with specifics for proof.

### I. Yes-Set / Utilization
Stack 3-4 undeniable statements, then the ask. Agreement momentum carries into the CTA.
> "You want results. You don't want to waste money. You want it simple. Then this is for you."

### Milton copy stack (ready template)
1. Mind-read open → 2. Pace 3 truths, lead → 3. Embedded commands through the benefits → 4. Presuppose the action
("when you start your trial…") → 5. Cause-effect benefit chain → 6. Tag question to close a section →
7. Double-bind CTA.

## 2. The Meta Model (precision — two uses)
**(A) Defensive:** strip these from YOUR claims so they feel specific and credible.
**(B) Offensive:** plant certain patterns to install belief below conscious resistance.

- **Universal quantifiers** (all, every, never, always): use on the BENEFIT side ("you'll never stare at a blank page again"), never on a falsifiable factual claim.
- **Modal operators** (must, should, can't / can, possible): "You shouldn't have to choose between speed and quality." "Imagine what becomes possible when…"
- **Complex equivalence** (X means Y): equate product with desired identity. "Choosing us means choosing peace of mind."
- **Mind reading** (offensive): see Milton §D.
- **Lost performative** (value as universal fact): "Good design isn't optional anymore." Authority by omission.
- **Cause-effect** (engine of benefit copy): "Click below and watch your conversions climb."
- **Nominalizations** (frozen verbs: freedom, transformation): vague, dreamy, perfect for aspiration; back with specifics.
- **Unspecified verbs/nouns:** kill in claims ("improves results" → "cuts load time 4s→0.8s"); use offensively for a projected antagonist ("they told you it couldn't be done").

## 3. Sleight of Mouth — 14 objection-reframe patterns (Dilts)
For each top objection, stack 2-4 of these into a short copy block. Example objection: **"Too expensive."**
1. **Intention** — "You're careful with budget, the exact mindset that makes this pay off."
2. **Redefine** — "It's not expensive, it's priced. Cheap tools cost you the 10 hours/week you lose now."
3. **Consequence** — "Choosing cheapest is why most teams re-buy three times."
4. **Chunk Down** — "Expensive vs what? It's €4 a day, less than your coffee."
5. **Chunk Up** — "This isn't a software cost, it's a growth decision. Against the revenue it unlocks, it's a rounding error."
6. **Counterexample** — "The teams with the best ROI almost didn't buy because of price. Now they call it the cheapest decision they made."
7. **Analogy** — "You'd never pick the cheapest surgeon. Same logic for the system running your revenue."
8. **Apply to Self** — "If price is your filter, you already agree value beats the lowest number. That's the case for buying this."
9. **Another Outcome** — "The real question isn't whether it's expensive, it's whether you'll still be doing this manually in six months."
10. **Hierarchy of Criteria** — "Isn't reliability worth more than saving a few euros?"
11. **Change Frame Size** — "Over a year that's €1,400 for 500 hours back. Trade 500 hours for €1,400?"
12. **Meta Frame** — "'Too expensive' usually means the value isn't clear yet. Here it is, line by line."
13. **Model of the World** — "Top operators don't ask 'cheapest?', they ask 'best tool for the job?'"
14. **Reality Strategy** — "How are you deciding it's expensive: against the price, or against the return?"

Best combos: Redefine+ChunkUp+Consequence (price), Counterexample+RealityStrategy (skepticism),
Intention+AnotherOutcome (procrastination), ModelOfWorld+HierarchyOfCriteria (status buyers).

## 4. Reframing (turn objections into benefits without disputing the fact)
- **Context reframe** ("bad here, asset elsewhere"): "Too simple?" → "Simple is exactly why your whole team adopts it in a day instead of fighting a bloated tool for months."
- **Content/meaning reframe** ("doesn't mean what you think"): "I've tried tools like this." → "Then you already know what to avoid, which is why you'll recognise immediately how different this is."
- **Preframe (inoculation):** raise + reframe the objection before the reader voices it. "You might think this is just another tool. Here's why it isn't…"
- Mechanics: attack the complex-equivalence or cause-effect the objection rests on ("expensive = bad" → break it).

## 5. Anchoring (install & fire states in sequential copy/UX)
A stimulus paired with a peak state re-fires that state later. 4 keys: **uniqueness, intensity (set at the PEAK),
purity (one clean emotion), timing.**
- Build the peak positive state FIRST (vivid outcome, relief), then attach brand/color/CTA at the crest.
- **Never anchor the brand to the pain section.** Problem/agitate = no brand. Solution reveal + dream = brand + CTA.
- One signature accent color on every CTA, one recurring phrase = repeatable anchor that fires on sight.
- Stack anchors: testimonial + result number + guarantee badge clustered at the CTA compound the pull.
- Spatial/layout anchor: testimonials always right of the claim, CTA always same position. Layout becomes the anchor.

## 6. Submodalities (vivid sensory language that shifts internal experience)
The brain can't fully distinguish a vividly imagined experience from a real one. The qualities of an internal image
(submodalities) set its emotional intensity. This is why "show, don't tell" works neurologically.
- **Visual dials:** brightness, size, distance, color vs B&W, focus, motion, associated (through own eyes) vs dissociated.
- **Auditory dials:** volume, pitch, tempo, location, tone.
- **Kinesthetic dials:** location in body, temperature, pressure, texture, weight, movement.
- **To intensify desire:** describe the dream outcome MAXED, big/bright/close/color/moving/warm, associated ("you're there, feeling it").
- **To dissolve a fear/objection:** shrink it, small/distant/dim/grey/frozen/silent ("that nagging worry, fading to a distant grey little thing").

## 7. Quick copy-assembly order (canonical neuro-arc)
1. Pattern interrupt / hook → 2. Mind-read open (rapport) → 3. Pace 3 truths, lead → 4. Agitate away-from pain
(no brand anchor) → 5. Reframe/preframe objections (Sleight of Mouth) → 6. Solution reveal at the emotional turn
(anchor brand here) → 7. Toward-dream, submodalities maxed, VAK predicates → 8. Embedded commands + presuppositions
throughout → 9. Multi-channel convincer proof (see/hear/read/do) → 10. Future-pace the win →
11. Tag-question + double-bind CTA (anchored to peak state).
